The PIPE TYPES of New York City

by: Eric Rothman

New York City has weird pipes sticking out of it everywhere:

Have you ever noticed them? Stopped to wonder what they're for? What secrets do they carry hither and thither inside their tubular forms?

New Yorkers are famously good at ignoring their surroundings–but songwriter Mac Davis thought slowing down to ponder the world was important:

Hey Mister
Where you going in such a hurry
Don't you think it's time you realized
There's a whole lot more to life than work and worry

When he mentioned the sweetest things in life being free and right before your eyes, he was almost certainly talking about pipes.

Opportunities to pipe-spot are abundant. Standpipes, a type of pipe we'll examine below, are mandatory fixtures on buildings taller than 6 stories. There are 7,659 such buildings in Manhattan:

That's 7,659 standpipes you could theoretically be appreciating. And that's just one type of pipe, in one borough! Compare that to how many pipes you're currently appreciating:

To save you from a life of pipe-blindness—and to single-handedly raise the city's collective appreciation metrics—I took to the streets to peep some pipes.

To keep things under control, I limited my data collection to pipes meeting to the following criteria:

  • externally terminating pipes
  • visible from the street
  • at the first story or below
  • kinda neat in some way

I walked a meandering 7,036 meters (4.37 miles) over a period of 2 hours and 46 minutes looking for pipes. I counted 132. That's 47.8 pipes per hour!

1. Standpipes

Standpipes are for fighting fires in places that are prohibitively far from fire hydrants (i.e. high up in tall buildings). Instead of lugging an extremely long hose up 19 floors, firefighters connect hydrant water to a building's standpipe system. Water flows from the siamese connection at the street vertically up a pipe in the building's stairwell, providing access to high-pressure water at every floor.

Some standpipes connect water to a building's sprinkler system as well. If you see a standpipe connector painted green, it's for sprinklers:

Yellow standpipes indicate a combination system. Googly eyes indicate a silly disposition:

2. Oil Tank Fill And Vent Pipes

It might sound old fashioned, but even today in NYC, many residential buildings get heat and hot water by burning oil in the basement. An oil tank refill pipe extends from a tank underground all the way up to the street:

A separate vent pipe, usually somewhere near the fill pipe, allows air to escape during refilling. It's most recognizable by a distinct mushroom-shaped vent cap which prevents city detritus from getting inside.

In contrast to the prideful standpipe, oil fill and vent pipes seem keen on hiding. They often take on the character of their surroundings:

Despite best efforts to blend in, they attract stickers and, occasionally, cups.

The combustion of oil is now prohibited as a primary heating source in new construction (though it is still allowed as a backup). Modern building codes also now require that vents terminate much higher from the street surface, so you'll be more likely to spot these old-fashioned pipes near older residential buildings.

3. Fresh Air Inlets

These pipes were first required by the city back in 1881. The prevailing theory at the time was that foul-smelling sewer gas from the main city sewer pipes directly caused disease. To protect the home, plumbers installed a trap on the sewer line and added this fresh air inlet just in front of it to let a building's internal plumbing breathe.

In theory, this is all important and good. In practice, it ignited a fierce, decades-long engineering debate. Experts quickly realized that when someone flushed a toilet on an upper floor, the water acted like a piston pushing down the pipe. Instead of passively drawing fresh air in, the inlet would suddenly blast foul air out.

According to Mechanics of the Household by E. S. Keene (published in 1911):

“...the descending water forces out the air of the pipe ahead of it, and a gush of offensive air filled with sewer gases is forced out of the vent. It is evident that such a vent, located near an open window or where it will reach the nostrils of the inhabitants is a thing not greatly to be desired.”

If you have ever thought that New York City smells bad, these pipes could be a culprit. Remarkably, despite experts like Keene pointing out this glaring, smelly flaw as early as 1911, bureaucratic inertia and squabbling factions of plumbers kept the fresh air inlet in the city's building code until 1960.

Understandably, like oil pipes, they are often visually encouraged into the background.

You'll often see these pipes in pairs, indicating separate systems for venting rain runoff and household plumbing into the city sewer.

This pair seems especially intimate:

Despite their offensive purpose, they are nearly ubiquitous near buildings built or renovated before 1960, when they were last required by city's plumbing code. Nowadays, everything vents up on the roof–out of smelling range, out of mind.

4. Pipes With Boxes on Top of Them

At first, I thought this was just a weird storage solution someone DIY'd outside of a bodega. After spotting a second one, I decided to investigate. Opening the lid, in addition to some garbage, I found an oil fill pipe inside! Maybe it doesn't deserve its own category after all! Why the extra security though? We may never know.

5. Steamy Street Pipes

No New York City pipespotting effort would be complete without noting the iconic, orange and white, steamy street pipe. Despite seeming ominous or even gross to some, the steam pouring out of these pipes comes from a vast underground steam delivery network run by Con Edison. It heats, cools, and provides hot water to large buildings throughout the city. It's not gross!

Steam is not meant to be pouring out of the street, however. If it is, it indicates either a leak in the system or cold water contacting the hot steam delivery pipes somewhere underground. If you see an orange and white pipe on top, it means Con Edison is aware of the issue. The pipes themselves help direct steam away from the street so drivers can see up ahead. Also, because steam is hot, they prevent people from getting burned!

It's hard to pinpoint exactly where to find one of these as they crop up randomly, but a good place to look is in the middle of the street.
